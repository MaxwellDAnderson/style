Style & Citation Manual

Datelines

Datelines at the beginning of a letter or paper are set at the right side of the page, the originating office in caps and small caps, the address and date in italics. Thus:

The White House,   
Washington, DC, January 1, 2016. 

If the originating office is not given, then the address is set in caps and small caps and the date in italic. Thus:

The White House, July 30, 2016. 

If only the date is given, then the date is set in caps and small caps. Thus:

January 24, 2016.

Words & Phrases

Groundwater vs. Ground Water
There is an ongoing dispute about whether the proper spelling of this term is one or two words. The contemporary consensus is the one-word form. The evolution of this consensus is documented in a pair of memoranda the United States Geological Survey issued. Compare U.S. Geological Survey, Ground Water Branch, Technical Memorandum GW1975.03, Re: Ground water versus groundwater (Aug. 22, 1974) (concluding that “ground water” is correct), with U.S. Geological Survey, Technical Memorandum GW2009.03, Re: Ground water vs. groundwater (Mar. 26, 2009) (concluding that “groundwater” is correct).
Notwithstanding the contemporary consensus, there are times when use of the two-word form is preferable.

Case Names

United States Reports

The first 90 volumes of the United States Reports (U.S.) were originally cited with reference to the Reporter of Decisions. This was because the first ninety volumes were privately published. In 1874, Congress began to fund the publication of what is now the U.S. Reports. The first ninety volumes were renumbered retroactively. While the first ninety volumes may be cited without reference to the Reporter, e.g., Marbury v. Madison, 5 U.S. 137 (1803), The Bluebook recommends the citation to include a parenthetical reference to the Reporter.
